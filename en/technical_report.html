NOTEBOOK 01

Data & Elo System

Data Source

Kaggle dataset (martj42/international-football-results-from-1872-to-2017), updated through 2026. Contains 49,287 matches including the 72 World Cup 2026 fixtures with no result yet.

Decision: time window 2010–2026

Matches before 2010 were discarded for three reasons: pre-2010 football is a different process (tactics, physical level, globalization); Elo captures prior history in a single number; and stationarity within the window was verified (average goals oscillate between 1.4 and 1.7 with no systematic trend).

Custom Elo System

What is Elo and what does it do?

The Elo is a numerical rating system that measures the relative level of each team at a given point in time. It was invented by physicist Arpad Elo for chess and widely adopted in competitive sports. The core idea is simple: each team has a number that rises when they win and falls when they lose, but the change depends on how surprising the result was.

If Spain (Elo 2100) beats Curaçao (Elo 1550), the change is minimal — it was expected. If Curaçao beats Spain, the change is large for both — it was a surprise.

Why we use it: it is the most stable signal available for international football. It summarizes years of history in a single number, updates automatically based on results, and has demonstrated high predictive power. With Elo differences greater than 300 points, the favorite wins between 80% and 88% of matches in our data.

Why we compute it ourselves instead of using eloratings.net or other sources: full control over parameters (initial value, K factor), perfect coverage of all teams, and direct learning from the process.

Elo is updated match by match in strict chronological order, ensuring that only past information is used at the time of each match (no data leakage).

Update Formula
nuevo_elo = elo_actual + K × (resultado_real − resultado_esperado) resultado_esperado = 1 / (1 + 10^((elo_rival − elo_equipo) / 400))

K Factor — what does it control and why does it matter?

What is the K factor?

The K factor is the amplifier of each match's impact on the rating. It determines how many Elo points are transferred between teams as a result of a match.

With a high K, the system is reactive: a surprising result changes the rating significantly right away. With a low K, the system is stable: it takes many results to move the rating meaningfully.

Why we vary it by competition: not all matches carry the same informational value. A warm-up friendly says little about a team's true level (coaches rotate, no pressure). A World Cup match demands maximum effort and intensity. Using a uniform K would treat both equally, contaminating the rating with weak signal.

Features & Results Model

Why add features beyond Elo?

Elo captures accumulated level but has two limitations: it doesn't capture momentum (a streak of 5 wins vs 5 irregular wins produce the same Elo) and it doesn't capture margin of victory (a 7-0 and a 1-0 update Elo equally).

FeatureWhat it capturesWhat Elo misses
diff_eloRelative level between teamsBase feature
localiaReal home ground advantageNeutral vs non-neutral pitch
diff_puntos_recientesMomentum (last 5 matches)Trend, not just level
diff_goles_anotadosRecent attacking outputMargin of victory
diff_goles_recibidosRecent defensive solidityMargin of defeat

Critical decision: temporal split

Training: 2010–2022 (11,950 matches). Evaluation: 2023–2026 (3,442 matches). Random cross-validation is not used because time has a direction: mixing dates produces artificially good metrics (data leakage).

Chosen model: Multinomial Logistic Regression

Produces probabilities directly for 3 classes (Home Win / Draw / Away Win). It is interpretable, robust with moderate-sized datasets, and hard to beat with weak features. Random Forest or XGBoost require more data and features to justify their added complexity.

Evaluation Metrics — Log-Loss & Brier Score

Why not accuracy?

Accuracy (percentage of correct predictions) seems intuitive but is a poor metric for probabilistic models. A model that always predicts "the favourite wins" can have good accuracy yet be useless: it says nothing about uncertainty or how confident we should be in each case.

We want to know if probabilities are well calibrated: when the model says 30%, does the event occur close to 30% of the time? That is measured with log-loss and Brier score.

Log-Loss — penalizes overconfident wrong predictions

The log-loss measures how harshly the model punishes itself for wrong predictions. The formula uses logarithms, generating a penalty that grows exponentially when the model is very confident about something that didn't happen.

Example: if the model says "Argentina has a 95% chance of winning" and Argentina loses, the log-loss is very high. If it says "Argentina has 55% odds" and they lose, the log-loss is low.

Range: 0 = perfect prediction. No upper bound, but a model that always predicts the base distribution has log-loss ≈ 1.22 on our data. Our model achieved 0.867 — a real and significant improvement.

Brier Score — squared error of probabilities

The Brier Score is the equivalent of mean squared error (MSE) applied to probabilities. It measures the squared difference between the predicted probability and the actual outcome (0 or 1).

It's more intuitive than log-loss: if the model predicts 0.7 probability of a win and the team won, the error is (0.7 - 1)² = 0.09. If it predicted 0.3 and the team won, the error is (0.3 - 1)² = 0.49.

Range: 0 = perfect prediction, 1 = worst possible. A model that always predicts 0.5 has Brier ≈ 0.25. Our model achieved 0.170 — well below the naive baseline.

NOTEBOOK 03

Goals Model (Poisson)

Why a separate goals model?

The results model predicts win/draw/loss but says nothing about the scoreline. Tournament simulation requires concrete scores: a 1-0 and a 3-2 are both home wins but have different implications for goal difference in the group stage.

Why the Poisson distribution?

What is the Poisson distribution and what does it measure?

The Poisson distribution models the probability of a given number of events occurring in a time interval, given that events are independent and occur at a constant average rate (λ, lambda).

In football, goals are exactly that: discrete events (0, 1, 2, 3…), never negative, occurring at an average rate that depends on the teams. The average in our data is 1.36 goals per team per match — and the empirical distribution matches a Poisson with λ ≈ 1.36.

Why not linear regression: linear regression doesn't know goals can't be negative. It could predict -0.3 goals for a very weak team, which is meaningless. Poisson is designed for count data.

How we use it: the model estimates a lambda for each team in each match. We then sample actual goals from a Poisson(λ) distribution. This injects realistic randomness: with λ=1.4, sometimes the outcome is 0, sometimes 1, sometimes 3 — just like in reality.

Model Formula
λ = e^(β₀ + β₁×elo_equipo + β₂×elo_rival + β₃×es_local) # Estimated coefficients (Elo normalized /1000): elo_equipo: +2.013 # higher own Elo → more goals elo_rival: -2.279 # higher opponent Elo → fewer goals es_local: +0.249 # home team scores 28% more than away

The model reproduces the real goal distribution well: Poisson-simulated frequencies are nearly identical to actual ones for values from 0 to 7 goals. There is no systematic over- or under-estimation bias.
Known limitation: the independent Poisson model slightly underestimates zeros and overestimates ones. In V2, the Dixon-Coles model can be explored — it adds a correlation term to correct this bias in low-scoring results (0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 1-1).
NOTEBOOK 04

Monte Carlo Tournament Simulation

What is Monte Carlo simulation?

Monte Carlo — massive repetition of random scenarios

Monte Carlo simulation is a technique that solves complex problems by repeating a random experiment thousands of times and observing the distribution of outcomes. The name comes from the Monte Carlo casino, a symbol of chance.

Football is too complex a system to solve analytically: 48 teams, 12 groups, thousands of possible knockout brackets, and each match has three possible outcomes. It's impossible to directly calculate "what is the probability that Argentina and Spain meet in the semis?".

The Monte Carlo solution: instead of calculating, we simulate. We run the full tournament 10,000 times. In each simulation, every match is resolved by sampling a random outcome according to the model's probabilities. At the end, we count frequencies: if Argentina won the tournament in 1,187 of the 10,000 simulations, their estimated probability is 11.87%.

Why 10,000? With few simulations (100), estimates are noisy. With 10,000, variance is low enough that 1-2% differences between teams are statistically stable. For this level of precision, 10,000 is the sweet spot between quality and compute time.

Simulated Tournament Structure

PhaseDetail

QualificationTop 2 from each group (24) + 8 best third-placed = 32 qualifiers

¿Qué significan los números?

The probabilities are not predictions: they are relative frequencies across 10,000 possible scenarios. Spain at 19.7% means they won in 1,970 out of 10,000 simulations. The system produces an uncertainty distribution, not a winner.
